##Yuqing Liu
In this project, I gained valuable experience in both teamwork and individual practice. I contributed significantly across multiple core stages, especially in data splitting, model selection, and feature prediction.

I learned from the insufficient experience of the dispersion of the theme of the last project. At the beginning of getting the data set, I consulted the literature with Jing on the point of predicting the target variables, and confirmed the specific research topic background of the swifts migration. 

Compared with Project 1, I had more time to adopt a more rigorous and systematic approach. At each stage, I explored multiple methods to improve prediction accuracy. Given the time dependence and lag effects in the data, we carefully designed the train–test split and minimized information leakage to ensure more realistic model evaluation.

During the modelling process, I compared different models of the same type and also incorporated AutoML as a benchmark. The tuned XGBoost achieved better performance on some metrics and offered greater interpretability. This experience deepened my understanding of the trade-off between automated models and manually tuned models.

There are still some shortcomings in this project. 
In the establishment stage of the research topic, we initially selected the migration behaviour of swifts based on their sensitivity to temperature. However, subsequent literature review revealed that swift migration is not directly driven by temperature, but is more closely related to food availability (e.g., insect activity), which itself is highly temperature-dependent. This indicates that temperature affects swift migration indirectly. As a result, our analysis did not show the expected strong direct correlation between temperature and migration timing. This highlights that our initial understanding of the causal relationships between variables was insufficient, which limited the interpretability of the model results.
